economic growth, see Krueger and Lindahl and Wasmer et al..De la Fuente and Ciccone review the literature with specific reference to Europe.The so-called “Mincerian equation” specifies a relationship an individual’s education and experience and his or her wages.For en extensive review of the micro evidence, see Card .The importance of human capital externalities in the process of development has been stressed by Lucas and Azariadis and Drazen , among others.Empirical studies in the US have, however, failed to detect human capital externalities at the US state and city level .Moretti does provide some evidence of sizable externalities at the US plant level.Human capital also includes health.Yet accounting for health, while of major importance in emerging and under-developed countries, is most likely of minor importance for the industrial world.The Appendix Table reports some simple cross-country regressions illustrating this point.Models and report the coefficients on initial average years of schooling and schooling quality in explaining long-term growth in the period 1960-2000.While both human capital proxies enter with highly significant coefficients in specifications and , models and that jointly assess their effect show that schooling quality is a far more significant correlate of growth than schooling quantity.The estimated effects are similar if the analysis is confided to the periods 1970-2000, 1980-2000, or 1990-2000.There is evidence of increased share of the total hours worked by more educated workers and, in particular, of a sizeable increase in the share of hours worked by those with tertiary education.For a formalisation of this intuition, see Acemoglu, Aghion, and Zilibotti .The skill context of the recent technological revolution has also been a key force for raising inequality.If this pattern continues then it is of fundamental importance for Europe to further invest in human capital.Not only this would further sustain growth, but also help reduce social injustices.For the effect of computer adoption on wages and skill upgrading in the US, see also Autor, Katz and Krueger .Levine provides a thorough review of the literature.Papaioannou also surveys work focusing on studies that are mostly related to advanced economies.These figures draw on Ciccone and Papaioannou and Hartmann, Heider, Lo Duca, and Papaioannou and are based on a methodology proposed by Wurgler .Data taken from UNIDO .The OECD defines financial education as “ The process by which financial consumers/investors improve their understanding of financial products and concepts and, through information, instruction, and/or objective advice, develop the skills and confidence to become more aware of financial risks and opportunities to make informed choices, to know where to go for help, and to take other effective actions to improve their financial well-being."The importance of financial literacy for household finance is stressed also by Campbell in his Presidential Address to the American Finance Association.
